Pliny

Welcome! Pliny is a tool that works with annotations or notes that you gather as you are reading -- arguably the starting point for much personal scholarly research in the humanities. It can be used with both digital (web sites, images and PDF files) and non-digital (books, journal articles) materials. The text of the annotation can be about anything that you wish to record -- indeed, whatever strikes you about the material you are reading is fair game.

Pliny is designed assuming that you will accumulate many notes over time. This leads us to a possible second phase in the use of Pliny, where your note collection provides a base platform upon which you might develop some sort of interpretation of the materials they refer to. To this end, Pliny has components in it that go beyond annotation facilities to help you manage your notes and organise them, even if you have thousands of them to work through. In this way we hope Pliny will help you with the task of taking your reactions to your research materials and transforming them into an overarching scheme or structure into which most or all your notes can fit, and that the structure you express in Pliny will capture some important aspects of the interpretation you have developed.

There are other pieces of software that work with concepts and ideas in ways that are similar to Pliny, but they are not widely known, and we assume here that you are not familiar with them. Because what Pliny does is quite different from what software you are familiar with does (it is not a web browser, it is not a word processor, it is not an email program, etc.), you may find that you will have to work a bit to understand Pliny and to see what it can do for you. For this reason we provide here more than just reference material that is meant to remind you how to do things you already do with other programs. We also preface this with a large section that introduces you to Pliny in general. The documentation, then, breaks into two divisions:

  • The section "Getting Started with Pliny" introduces you to the Eclipse environment in which Pliny operates, and provides some tutorials that will help you start using Pliny to support your own work. If you are unfamiliar with software that does the kind of things that Pliny does for you, we suggest that you at least read through one or two of these before beginning to work with Pliny.
  • The section "Pliny Reference" provides a somewhat more formal description of the components that make up Pliny's user interface.

Pliny works on the Macintosh, but it looks a little different from how it looks under Windows. There is a little about the differences at Pliny on the Macintosh.

The Pliny project on the WWW

The Pliny project website can be found at http://pliny.cch.kcl.ac.uk. Go there to find out about updates to Pliny and other information about it, including any current news about the Pliny project. There are also some articles (listed near the end of webpage http://pliny.cch.kcl.ac.uk/whatIsPliny.html) that provide you with an overview of what Pliny is about.

In addition, the Pliny program is open-source, and is designed the way it is to facilitate collaboration between tool builders and the tools they build. If you are a tool builder for the Humanities, you might be interested in some of the information provided in the developers section of the project website as well.
